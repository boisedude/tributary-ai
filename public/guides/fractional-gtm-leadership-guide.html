1. What is Fractional GTM Leadership?

Fractional GTM (Go-To-Market) leadership is a model where experienced executives work with your company on a part-time, ongoing basis—typically 10-20 hours per week. Unlike traditional consultants who provide advice and leave, fractional leaders embed themselves within your organization, taking ownership of strategy execution and team development.

The 2026 Fractional Leadership Landscape

The fractional executive market has matured significantly. Key trends for 2026:

  • Mainstream adoption: Nearly 37% of mid-sized firms plan to employ fractional or interim executives by mid-2026, up from just 12% in 2020
  • Market growth: The number of fractional leaders more than doubled from around 60,000 in 2022 to over 120,000 in 2024, with continued growth into 2026
  • Long-term partnerships: Fractional executives are no longer viewed as interim solutions—they are increasingly embedded as long-term contributors to strategy and execution
  • AI-native expectations: The best fractional sales leaders in 2026 bring AI expertise to help build sales engines that operate at 3-4x the efficiency of competitors
  • Outcome-based engagements: Organizations now start fractional work by identifying specific challenges to resolve rather than assigning traditional job positions

Common Fractional GTM Roles

Fractional Chief Revenue Officer (CRO): Owns the entire revenue engine—sales, marketing, customer success, and partnerships. Sets strategy, builds processes, and ensures alignment across all revenue-generating functions. Ideal for companies transitioning from founder-led sales or scaling beyond $3M ARR.

Fractional VP of Sales: Focuses specifically on building and scaling the sales organization. Develops sales methodology, implements CRM processes, creates compensation plans, and coaches reps. Best for companies with product-market fit ready to formalize their sales function.

Fractional VP of Partnerships: Establishes and scales strategic partnerships, channel programs, and ecosystem relationships. Navigates cloud marketplace listings (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud), integration partnerships, and reseller channels. Critical for SaaS companies with partnership-led growth strategies.

Fractional VP of Revenue Operations: Builds the systems, processes, and analytics infrastructure to support scalable revenue growth. Implements tech stack, creates dashboards, establishes forecasting models, and optimizes conversion funnels. Essential when sales ops has become too complex for part-time attention.

How Fractional Executives Embed with Your Team

A fractional leader doesn't just advise from afar. They:

  • Attend regular team meetings and 1:1s with reports
  • Have direct access to your CRM, tools, and systems
  • Make hiring decisions and participate in interviews
  • Run deal reviews and forecast calls
  • Represent your company in customer and partner meetings
  • Create and own strategic initiatives with measurable KPIs

They function as a true member of your leadership team, just on a part-time schedule.

2. When to Consider Fractional Leadership

Fractional GTM leadership makes sense at specific inflection points in your company's growth. Understanding when you're ready—and when you're not—can save you time and money.

Stage and Funding Considerations

Seed Stage ($500K-$2M ARR): You likely don't need fractional leadership yet unless you have a unique situation (experienced founder stepping back from sales, rapid unexpected growth, complex partnership deals). Focus on finding product-market fit first.

Series A ($2M-$10M ARR): The sweet spot for fractional leadership. You've proven product-market fit and need to formalize your GTM motion, but can't yet justify $400K+ for a full-time CRO or VP Sales. A fractional leader can build your foundation for 25-35% of the cost. This is also when many companies hire their first VP Sales (typically $1M-$5M ARR range).

Series B ($10M-$30M ARR): Fractional leaders excel at specific functions you haven't scaled yet—partnerships, customer success operations, new market entry. You might have a full-time VP Sales but need fractional expertise for cloud marketplace strategy or channel development. Note: Most companies don't hire a CRO until $15M-$20M+ ARR.

Series C and Beyond ($30M+ ARR): Fractional leadership works best for specialized initiatives: international expansion, new product line GTM, marketplace optimization, AI operations strategy, or M&A integration. Your core GTM team should be full-time at this stage. CROs typically come in at $20M-$50M ARR to lead the entire go-to-market function.

Signs You Need GTM Leadership But Can't Justify Full-Time

  • Founders are still running all sales, partnerships, or customer success activities
  • You have SDRs and AEs but no consistent methodology or forecasting
  • Your sales team isn't hitting quota, but you don't know why
  • You're getting partnership inquiries but have no one to manage them strategically
  • Marketing generates leads that sales can't convert effectively
  • Your board is asking about revenue predictability and you don't have good answers
  • You need to hire GTM talent but don't know what "good" looks like
  • A full-time executive would cost 15%+ of your annual revenue (at $5M ARR, that's $400K+)

Common Scenarios for Fractional Engagement

Founder-Led Sales Transition: You've closed your first $2-5M in ARR yourself, but you need to build a repeatable sales process and hire your first sales team. A fractional VP Sales can establish methodology, create playbooks, and hire/train your initial reps while transferring knowledge.

New Market Entry: You're successful in North America and want to expand to Europe or APAC. A fractional leader with regional expertise can establish your presence, navigate local nuances, and validate the opportunity before committing to a full-time regional leader.

Cloud Marketplace Launch: Getting listed on AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud Marketplace requires specialized knowledge. A fractional VP Partnerships can navigate the technical and business requirements, negotiate co-sell agreements, and establish the foundation for marketplace-led growth.

Sales Team Turnaround: Your team consistently misses quota and turnover is high. A fractional CRO can diagnose the issues (wrong ICP, poor process, compensation problems, lack of coaching) and implement fixes without the long-term commitment of a full-time hire.

Interim Leadership: Your VP Sales left unexpectedly. Rather than rushing a full-time hire or leaving the team rudderless, a fractional leader can step in immediately, stabilize the function, and help you find the right permanent replacement.

When Fractional Leadership ISN'T the Answer

Be honest about these situations:

  • No product-market fit: Fractional leaders can't sell what doesn't work. Get your ICP and value proposition right first.
  • Lack of budget for any execution: If you can't afford the tools, headcount, or programs they'll recommend, wait until you can.
  • Need for 40+ hours/week: If the role truly requires full-time attention, hire full-time. Fractional works for strategic leadership, not constant firefighting.
  • Cultural resistance: If your team won't accept outside leadership or you're not willing to implement recommended changes, save your money.

Pros and Cons of Each Model

Fractional Leadership - Best For:

  • Series A companies ($2M-$10M ARR) that need senior expertise
  • Specific GTM functions you haven't built yet (partnerships, rev ops)
  • Interim coverage while recruiting full-time
  • Testing new market or GTM strategies before full commitment
  • Companies with budget constraints but urgent GTM needs

Fractional Leadership - Drawbacks:

  • Limited availability (can't handle constant emergencies)
  • May work with other companies (though never competitors)
  • Not ideal for functions requiring 40+ hours of attention weekly
  • Team may initially be skeptical of "part-time" leadership

Full-Time Executive - Best For:

  • Series B+ companies with proven GTM and strong revenue ($10M+ ARR)
  • Core functions requiring daily attention and management
  • Building long-term institutional knowledge
  • Roles requiring 40+ hours of weekly focus
  • Companies with budget for $400K+ total compensation

Full-Time Executive - Drawbacks:

  • 2-6 month hiring process (opportunity cost)
  • 3-4x more expensive than fractional
  • High cost if the hire doesn't work out (8 out of 10 first VP Sales hires fail)
  • Less flexibility to adjust scope or scale down

Traditional Consulting - Best For:

  • Specific projects with defined scope and timeline
  • Strategic analysis and recommendations
  • Board-level planning exercises
  • Situations where you have the team to execute but need expert guidance

Traditional Consulting - Drawbacks:

  • Recommendations sit on a shelf without execution support
  • Limited accountability for outcomes
  • Knowledge leaves when the project ends
  • Expensive relative to value if you need ongoing leadership

4. What to Expect from a Fractional Engagement

Setting realistic expectations upfront ensures a successful partnership. Here's what a well-structured fractional engagement looks like in practice.

Typical Time Commitment

Most fractional executives work 10-20 hours per week, structured around your team's needs:

10 Hours/Week (Minimum Viable):

  • 2-3 days of availability, typically specific weekdays
  • Strategic oversight, coaching, and key decision-making
  • Weekly team meetings and 1:1s with direct reports
  • Monthly/quarterly planning and board prep
  • Best for: Advisory role with strong execution team in place

15 Hours/Week (Sweet Spot):

  • 3-4 days of availability with predictable schedule
  • Strategic leadership plus hands-on execution
  • Direct team management, deal reviews, customer meetings
  • Process building, playbook creation, training
  • Best for: Active leadership role building or scaling a function

20 Hours/Week (Maximum Fractional):

  • 4-5 days of availability, almost indistinguishable from full-time
  • Full leadership responsibility for the function
  • Multiple direct reports, active hiring, deep execution
  • Best for: Interim coverage or intensive transformation work

Sample Weekly Schedule (15 hours)

Monday (3 hours): Week planning, deal reviews, pipeline analysis

Tuesday (4 hours): 1:1s with direct reports, customer/partner calls

Wednesday (3 hours): Team meeting, process improvement work, async communication

Thursday (3 hours): Strategic projects, board prep, hiring interviews

Friday (2 hours): Week review, planning for next week, documentation

Engagement Duration

Initial Engagement: 3-6 months is typical, structured as:

  • Month 1: Discovery, assessment, quick wins. Understanding current state, identifying gaps, building relationships, implementing 1-2 immediate improvements.
  • Months 2-3: Foundation building. Implementing core processes, hiring key roles, training team, establishing metrics and reporting.
  • Months 4-6: Optimization and scale. Refining what's working, addressing gaps, scaling successful initiatives, knowledge transfer.

Market data shows average fractional assignments now run 9.7 months, with weekly time commitments averaging 14.6 hours—indicating stronger integration into client organizations than in previous years.

Renewal and Extension: Many fractional engagements extend 6-18 months as needs evolve:

  • Continue at current level while scaling initiatives
  • Reduce hours as team builds capability (15 to 10 hours)
  • Shift focus to new function or market
  • Transition to advisory role (5 hours/month)
  • Convert to full-time if business scales and budget allows

Team Integration

A fractional leader embeds into your organization as a true executive:

Tools and Access:

  • Full access to CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot), email, Slack/Teams
  • Admin rights to GTM tools (Outreach, LinkedIn Sales Nav, etc.)
  • Financial visibility into budgets, compensation, and metrics
  • Company email address and signature

Authority and Decision-Making:

  • Direct management of assigned team members
  • Hiring authority (usually with CEO/founder approval)
  • Budget ownership for their functional area
  • Strategic decision-making within defined scope
  • Representation in customer, partner, and investor meetings

Cultural Integration:

  • Participates in all-hands meetings and team events
  • Listed on org chart with clear reporting lines
  • Introduced to customers and partners as part of leadership
  • Receives company updates and context

Communication and Reporting Cadence

Weekly:

  • 1:1 with CEO/founder (30-60 minutes)
  • Team meeting with direct reports
  • Pipeline/forecast review
  • Async updates in Slack/email on progress and blockers

Monthly:

  • Written update on KPIs and progress against goals
  • Strategic planning and priority-setting session
  • Budget and forecast review
  • Board deck contribution (if applicable)

Quarterly:

  • Comprehensive business review
  • Goal-setting for next quarter (OKRs/KPIs)
  • Strategic planning and prioritization
  • Engagement review: what's working, what needs adjustment

First 30 Days: What Success Looks Like

A great fractional leader will accomplish the following in their first month:

  • Week 1: Meet all key stakeholders, review all existing documentation, identify 2-3 quick wins
  • Week 2: Deep dive into data (CRM, metrics, pipeline), interview customers and team members, present initial findings
  • Week 3: Implement first quick win, create 90-day plan with priorities and KPIs, establish communication cadence
  • Week 4: Begin executing on plan, hire or train if needed, establish baseline metrics and reporting

You should see tangible value by Day 30, not just planning documents.

5. Finding and Evaluating Fractional Leaders

The quality of your fractional leader makes all the difference. Here's how to find, evaluate, and select the right person for your needs.

What to Look for in a Fractional Executive

Domain Expertise:

  • SaaS Experience: Have they sold or led sales in SaaS specifically? SaaS sales motions differ fundamentally from other B2B models.
  • Stage Fit: Series A needs are different from Series C. Find someone who's succeeded at your stage and the next one.
  • GTM Motion Match: PLG, sales-led, partner-led? Ensure they've built the motion you need.
  • Market Knowledge: Do they understand your ICP, buyer personas, and competitive landscape?

Execution Track Record:

  • Specific outcomes achieved (revenue growth, team scaling, process implementation)
  • Experience building 0→1 vs. scaling 1→10
  • Evidence of hands-on work, not just strategy
  • Multiple successful fractional or interim engagements (if experienced fractional)

Leadership and Communication:

  • Ability to coach and develop team members
  • Strong written and verbal communication (critical for part-time role)
  • Collaborative vs. dictatorial style (fit with your culture)
  • Comfort with ambiguity and startup pace

Fractional Mindset:

  • Self-directed and autonomous (won't need constant supervision)
  • Efficient with time (can drive impact in limited hours)
  • Excellent at knowledge transfer and documentation
  • Ego-free approach (here to enable success, not build empires)
  • Comfortable working alongside other fractionals or advisors

2026 Essential: AI and Technology Proficiency

  • Excited about using AI to drive sales efficiency—not to avoid selling
  • Experience implementing AI-powered sales tools and workflows
  • Understanding of how AI changes prospecting, qualification, and deal analysis
  • Can bring or recommend an "AI-native" operator for day-to-day execution
  • Track record of building teams that leverage automation effectively

Interview Questions to Ask

Experience and Fit:

  • "Walk me through a situation where you built [sales/partnerships/rev ops] from scratch. What were the first three things you did?"
  • "What's your experience with companies at our stage ($X ARR) and GTM motion?"
  • "Tell me about a fractional engagement that didn't go well. What happened and what did you learn?"
  • "What tools and tech stack do you typically implement? Why those?"

Approach and Methodology:

  • "If you started with us Monday, what would your first 30 days look like?"
  • "How do you prioritize when there are more problems than time to solve them?"
  • "What metrics would you focus on first for our stage and GTM?"
  • "How do you ensure knowledge transfer so we're not dependent on you long-term?"

Working Style and Expectations:

  • "How do you typically structure your weekly schedule across clients?"
  • "What do you need from us to be successful in a fractional role?"
  • "How do you handle urgent issues that come up outside your scheduled hours?"
  • "What does 'good' look like at 90 days? 6 months?"

Specific Scenarios:

  • "Our sales team is missing quota by 30%. How would you diagnose and fix that?"
  • "We need to hire our first two AEs. What's your hiring process?"
  • "How would you approach getting us listed on AWS Marketplace with co-sell?"
  • "We're founder-led sales today. How would you transition that to a sales team?"

AI and Technology (Critical for 2026):

  • "How have you leveraged AI tools to improve sales team productivity in your recent engagements?"
  • "What AI-powered tools would you recommend for our stage and GTM motion?"
  • "How do you balance AI automation with human relationship-building in the sales process?"
  • "What does an AI-native sales operation look like, and how would you build one here?"

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Vague answers: Can't provide specific examples, metrics, or methodologies
  • One-size-fits-all approach: Same solution for every company regardless of stage/context
  • Too many clients: Working with 5+ companies simultaneously (can't give you enough focus)
  • No references or poor references: Especially from CEOs at similar-stage companies
  • Lack of recent experience: Last GTM leadership role was 5+ years ago
  • No clear process: Can't articulate how they'll drive results in limited hours
  • Promises quick fixes: Real transformation takes months, not weeks
  • Unwilling to take accountability: Frames themselves as "advisor" not "owner"
  • Can't start for 2+ months: Suggests they're overcommitted or not serious

References and Due Diligence

Ask for 3-4 references, specifically:

  • A CEO/founder from a recent fractional engagement (last 12 months)
  • A direct report they managed or hired
  • A peer executive they worked alongside
  • A customer or partner they interacted with (for credibility check)

Reference Check Questions:

  • "What specific outcomes did [candidate] drive during their engagement?"
  • "How did they handle setbacks or things that didn't work?"
  • "Did they transfer knowledge effectively to your team?"
  • "Would you hire them again or extend the engagement? Why/why not?"
  • "What would you do differently if you worked with them again?"
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate their impact? Why that number?"

Additional Due Diligence:

  • LinkedIn profile review (recommendations, background verification)
  • Check for online presence (blog, speaking, thought leadership—shows they share knowledge)
  • Request sample work products (playbooks, dashboards, process docs from past engagements)
  • Trial project (small paid engagement before full commitment)

Where to Find Fractional Leaders

  • Direct Networks: Ask your investors, advisors, and peer CEOs for referrals
  • LinkedIn: Search "Fractional CRO" or "Fractional VP Sales" + your industry
  • Specialized Firms: Companies that offer fractional GTM leadership (like Tributary AI Systems)
  • Communities: SaaStr, Pavilion, GTM Slack groups, RevGenius
  • Fractional Marketplaces: Platforms connecting fractional executives with companies
  • Executive Recruiters: Some now place fractional leaders, not just full-time

6. Maximizing Value from Fractional Leadership

The success of a fractional engagement depends as much on what you do as what they do. Here's how to set up for maximum impact.

Setting Clear Objectives and KPIs

Establish 3-5 specific, measurable goals for the engagement upfront. Vague goals ("improve sales") lead to vague outcomes.

Good Goal Examples:

  • "Increase monthly new logo acquisition from 3 to 7 by end of Q2"
  • "Hire and ramp 2 AEs to 80%+ of quota within 90 days"
  • "Achieve AWS Marketplace listing with co-sell by month 4"
  • "Implement forecasting with <15% variance from actual by month 3"
  • "Improve sales cycle from 90 to 60 days for SMB segment"
  • "Build partnership pipeline generating 3+ qualified deals/month"

Bad Goal Examples (Too Vague):

  • "Help with sales strategy" (What does success look like?)
  • "Improve team performance" (How measured? By when?)
  • "Build partnerships" (How many? What kind? What outcomes?)

The 90-Day Plan Framework

Work with your fractional leader to create a clear 90-day plan in their first week:

Month 1: Assess and Stabilize

  • 2-3 quick wins to build credibility and momentum
  • Complete assessment of current state
  • Establish baseline metrics
  • Identify critical gaps and priorities

Month 2: Build and Implement

  • Deploy core processes and tools
  • Hire or train key team members
  • Launch 1-2 major initiatives
  • Establish reporting and governance

Month 3: Optimize and Scale

  • Refine what's working, fix what's not
  • Begin knowledge transfer and documentation
  • Expand successful programs
  • Plan for months 4-6 or transition

Ensuring Knowledge Transfer

The best fractional leaders make themselves unnecessary over time by building capability in your team.

Documentation Requirements:

  • Playbooks: Step-by-step guides for key processes (sales methodology, partnership playbooks, onboarding)
  • Templates: Reusable resources (email sequences, pitch decks, deal reviews, forecasts)
  • Decision Frameworks: How to evaluate deals, partnerships, hires, pricing
  • Training Materials: Recorded sessions, onboarding guides, coaching resources

Building Team Capability:

  • Shadow opportunities: Team members observe fractional leader in action
  • Reverse shadowing: Leader observes team and provides coaching
  • Delegation with oversight: Team leads initiatives with fractional leader's support
  • Weekly coaching sessions focused on skill development
  • Creating internal experts who can train others

Measuring Knowledge Transfer:

  • Can team members execute key processes without fractional leader?
  • Is there documentation that a new hire could learn from?
  • Have team members successfully trained others?
  • Could the function continue if the fractional leader left tomorrow?

Planning for Transition

Every fractional engagement should have a clear endgame from the start. Three common paths:

Path 1: Transition to Full-Time Hire

  • Fractional leader helps define role, recruit, and interview candidates
  • They onboard and train the new full-time executive
  • 30-60 day overlap period for knowledge transfer
  • Option: Fractional leader stays on in advisory capacity (5 hours/month)

Path 2: Team Becomes Self-Sufficient

  • Processes are documented and embedded
  • Team has capability to execute independently
  • Gradually reduce fractional hours (15→10→5→0)
  • Option: On-call support for quarterly planning or special projects

Path 3: Ongoing Partnership

  • Fractional leader continues in strategic capacity long-term
  • Scope evolves (e.g., from sales to partnerships, or expansion to new market)
  • Hours may adjust based on needs
  • Some functions (partnerships, rev ops) work well fractional long-term

What You Must Provide for Success

Your fractional leader needs these things from you:

  • Clear Authority: Team knows they report to the fractional leader; no undermining
  • Access to Information: Full transparency into financials, strategy, challenges
  • Decision Rights: Ability to make decisions within their scope without constant approval
  • Budget for Execution: If they recommend tools, headcount, or programs, you have budget
  • Regular CEO Time: Weekly 1:1s are non-negotiable; they need your context and input
  • Willingness to Change: If they identify problems, you'll implement solutions even if uncomfortable
  • Realistic Expectations: They can't fix years of problems in 10 hours/week in 30 days

7. Common Engagement Models and Pricing

Understanding how fractional engagements are structured helps you budget appropriately and negotiate fair terms.

Monthly Retainer Structures

The most common model for fractional leadership is a monthly retainer based on hours committed.

15 hours 60 hours $10,000-$18,000 $175-$300/hr Active leadership, building function

Note: 2026 market data shows average monthly compensation for fractional sales leaders at $11,732 with average hourly rates of $225, up from $9,651/month and $213/hour in 2024. Rates vary by experience, specialization, and geography.

What's Included in Retainer:

  • Committed weekly hours (predictable schedule)
  • All team meetings, 1:1s, and regular cadence activities
  • Email/Slack communication and async work
  • Access to their expertise and network
  • Standard tools they already use

What's Typically Extra:

  • Travel expenses (if on-site visits required)
  • Specialized tools or software they need to purchase
  • Work significantly beyond agreed hours (billed separately or negotiated)

Project-Based Engagements

Some fractional work is better structured as discrete projects with defined scope and deliverables.

Common Project-Based Examples:

  • AWS Marketplace Launch: $20K-$40K fixed fee for complete listing, co-sell application, and initial optimization (6-12 weeks)
  • Sales Methodology Implementation: $25K-$50K for MEDDPICC/Challenger/other methodology training and rollout (8-10 weeks)
  • Rev Ops Foundation: $30K-$60K for CRM optimization, reporting dashboards, forecasting model (8-12 weeks)
  • Go-to-Market Strategy: $20K-$45K for comprehensive GTM plan, ICP definition, pricing analysis (4-8 weeks)

Project vs. Retainer Decision:

  • Choose Project-Based when: Clear deliverable, defined timeline, one-time need, you have team to execute
  • Choose Retainer when: Ongoing leadership needed, team building/management required, evolving scope, long-term transformation

Pricing Red Flags

  • Too cheap: $5K-$6K/month for 15 hours means $80-100/hr—well below senior executive rates ($175-300/hr is standard in 2026)
  • Unclear scope: "We'll figure it out as we go" without defined hours or deliverables
  • Hourly-only pricing: Hard to budget, no commitment, can balloon unexpectedly. Note: 88% of fractional sales leaders use retainer-based compensation
  • All equity, no cash: "I'll work for equity only" usually means inexperienced or desperate
  • Upfront payment in full: 3-6 months paid in advance before work starts is risky

Equity Considerations

Equity for fractional leaders is less common than for full-time executives but increasingly normal, especially for longer engagements. In 2026, hybrid cash-equity structures are becoming more prevalent.

Typical Equity Ranges:

  • Short-term project (3-6 months): 0-0.1% (if any)
  • Standard fractional (6-12 months): 0.1-0.25%
  • Long-term fractional (12+ months): 0.25-0.5%
  • Fractional CRO with major impact: Up to 0.5-1.0% (rare)

For comparison: Full-time CROs typically receive 0.5-2.0% equity, with emerging CROs (at companies under $20M ARR) averaging 1.9% and enterprise CROs at public companies averaging 0.6%.

Vesting Structures:

  • Typically 2-year vesting (vs. 4-year for full-time)
  • Monthly vesting after 3-6 month cliff
  • Acceleration on change of control sometimes included
  • Options vs. restricted stock—usually options for fractional
  • Consider double-trigger vesting with time-based primary trigger and liquidity event secondary trigger

Cash vs. Equity Tradeoff:

  • Some fractional leaders will reduce cash rate in exchange for equity
  • Example: $14K/month + 0% equity OR $10K/month + 0.25% equity
  • Only makes sense if you're raising Series A+ and have reasonable valuation
  • Don't give equity just because you can't afford cash—find someone at your budget
  • Hybrid approaches (50/50 cash and equity split) are increasingly common for longer engagements

Contract Terms to Negotiate

Engagement Length:

  • Initial term: 3-6 months typical
  • Auto-renewal: Month-to-month after initial term, or 3-month renewals
  • Termination: 30 days notice by either party (after initial term)

Payment Terms:

  • Monthly invoicing, due net-15 or net-30
  • First month often due upfront before work begins
  • Quarterly payments sometimes offered at discount (save 5-10%)

Scope and Hours:

  • Clearly defined weekly hour commitment
  • Flexibility for occasional urgent needs (within reason)
  • How hours are tracked (if at all—many don't for senior roles)
  • Process for adjusting hours up or down

Exclusivity and Conflicts:

  • Non-compete for direct competitors (usually 1-2 year post-engagement)
  • Disclosure of other clients (transparency, not necessarily prohibition)
